# story_headline
The plan failed. The mission survived.

# story_body
In 1914, Ernest Shackleton set out to cross Antarctica.

He never made it.

His ship, the *Endurance*, got trapped in the ice long before they even reached land. For months the crew drifted with the frozen ocean, carried wherever the pack ice took them. Then, in one of the most remote places on Earth, the ice turned vicious. It slowly crushed the ship, grinding the hull until it gave way. Twenty-eight men watched their only home sink, left stranded on a frozen wasteland with no rescue in sight.

At that moment, everything changed.

The original mission died on the ice. The new one became simple: get every man home alive.

What followed was sixteen months of brutal cold, drifting ice floes, and an 800-mile journey across the roughest ocean on the planet in small wooden lifeboats.

The plan failed.
The mission survived.
**All twenty-eight men made it home.**

That's the part worth remembering. Shackleton did not run from uncertainty. He walked straight into it. His greatest win wasn't crossing Antarctica. It was refusing to leave anyone behind.

# idea_body
Here's why I'm telling you this.

A lot of you are stepping into your own unknown this summer. First internships, new cities, training blocks, or just trying to figure out what you actually want. Plans will shift. Some paths will disappear. That's not the detour. That's usually where the real story starts.

The good news? You're not drifting out there alone. There's a network of Cornell athlete alumni who were once exactly where you are. Nervous, ambitious, and figuring it out. Most of them are happy to talk.

You don't need the full map. You just need the next step. Find one alum. Send one message. Ask one real question. See what opens.

Endurance isn't reserved for explorers. It's the habit you've already built at 5 a.m. lifts, through two-a-days, and getting up after losses. The arena is changing. The athlete isn't.

The unknown is ahead.

**Good. That's where it begins.**
