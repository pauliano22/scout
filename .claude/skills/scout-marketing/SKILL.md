# Scout Marketing Content

Scout is an AI assistant that helps Cornell student-athletes find relevant alumni and draft outreach to them — the student always reviews before anything sends. The audience is Cornell student-athletes (and the alumni who mentor them). Marketing lives mostly on Instagram. This skill makes content that is on-brand *and* reads as written by a human.

Work in this order when producing a post: pick the pillar and format, write the headline/art copy, write the caption, then run the caption through the voice rules before finishing.

---

## 1. Voice rules — read these first, they break most often

The visual system keeps the *look* consistent. These keep the *writing* from reading as AI. Run every caption through this list before delivering it. This is the single highest-value part of the skill.

**Cut these tells:**
- **Em-dashes by the handful.** One per caption maximum. A comma or period almost always reads more human. The em-dash is the most recognized AI tell, so ration it hard.
- **"It's not X, it's Y."** This construction fakes the shape of insight without carrying any. Use it once a month across all content, not once a post. (e.g. avoid "It isn't luck. It's a pattern.")
- **The rule of three on repeat.** Stacking triplets ("Pick the alum. Pick the tone. Scout drafts it.") in caption after caption is a dead giveaway. Vary the count.
- **Uniform sentence length.** Write bursty: a long sentence, then a short one, then a medium one with an aside. Unevenness reads human; metronomic rhythm reads machine.
- **Tell-words:** delve, leverage, robust, seamless, elevate, unlock, pivotal, game-changer, "in today's landscape," "navigate the world of." Cut on sight.
- **Statement-then-restate padding.** Do not say a thing, then re-explain the thing you just said. Every sentence must add new information or get deleted.
- **Markdown italics or bold inside caption body copy.** Emphasis formatting in flowing text is a tell. (Emphasis in headline art is fine — see the exception below.)

**Do these instead:**
- **Be specific.** "A sophomore wrestler getting introduced to an alum who runs a desk" beats "athletes connecting with alumni." Concrete detail is the strongest signal a real person wrote it, and AI can't fabricate a real name, a real intro, or a real number from the dashboard. Lean on those.
- **Tell a micro-story.** The top-performing caption format is a 2–3 line true story, not a "3 tips" list. People scroll past lists now.
- **Pick a side.** No neutral both-sides hedging. Say the thing.
- **Front-load the hook.** It must land in the first ~80 characters, before the "...more" cutoff.
- **One concrete CTA.** "Tag a teammate who's been putting this off" beats "learn more."
- **Read it aloud.** If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it. This is the final test for every caption.

**The headline exception:** display copy — the big serif lines on the artwork — *can* keep tight parallelism and a single italic emphasis word ("Applications get ignored. Resumes get lost."). On a poster that is a deliberate device and it works. The tells only become tells when they pile up in flowing caption copy. Punch in the art; plain and human in the caption.

### Good vs. bad (calibration)

Bad (every tell at once):
> Scout writes the message. You decide if it sends. Pick the alum. Pick the tone. Scout drafts the intro — and nothing leaves your hands until you say so. It's not automation, it's leverage.

Good (specific, bursty, one CTA, no markdown emphasis):
> No, Scout doesn't send anything for you. That's the point. You pick who you want to reach, and Scout writes a first draft built around what you and that alum actually have in common, so you're not staring at an empty box. Then you read it, fix what sounds off, and send it yourself. See how it works at scoutcornell.com.

## 2. Brand & visual system — locked (Direction A: Modern Sans)

Never redesign this; reuse it. New artwork must match. The visual language is built **from the logo**, which is geometric sans — so the design is modern-sans, not editorial-serif.

- **Background:** warm cream / off-white (`#F5F1EC`-ish). Never pure white.
- **Typeface:** Inter everywhere, matching the wordmark. No serif display faces.
- **Headlines:** Inter, heavy (800–900), **left-aligned**, tight tracking (~-0.02em). Big and confident.
- **Emphasis:** by **weight and carnelian color only — never italics.** A key word or number goes carnelian; that is the whole emphasis system. (Big typographic numbers, e.g. `18,000`, are a signature move.)
- **Body / subheads:** Inter regular/medium, warm gray.
- **Accent color:** Cornell carnelian `#B31B1B`, one accent per slide. The restraint is the brand.
- **Logo:** the real Scout mark + `Scout` wordmark, top-left of every slide (assets in `~/Downloads/scout-logo/` — mark, horizontal, stacked, in SVG + PNG). Not a text lockup.
- **Ownable motif:** the logo's **red corner-cut** (a carnelian triangle in the top-right corner of the slide), reused as the recurring brand device instead of the old dot-grid.
- **Dividers:** hairlines only — no boxes, no heavy borders.
- **Footer:** `scoutcornell.com · free for Cornell athletes`, bottom of every slide.
- **Photography (best for looking un-AI):** when a real athlete/campus photo is available, run it full-bleed with a dark scrim and drop text to a clean lower-third, logo in white, one carnelian keyline. Real photos beat any built graphic.
- **Carousels:** end on a solid-carnelian CTA slide (white type, one button); keep pagination dots visible.

### Visual voice rules — keep the art from reading as AI (mirror of §1, for design)
Run new artwork past this list. These are the visual equivalents of the copy tells.
- **No borrowed elegant serif** (Playfair, Cormorant, etc.) — that centered-serif look is the #1 AI/Canva-template tell. Design from the logo (Inter).
- **No lone italic accent word.** Emphasis is weight + carnelian, never a single slanted word.
- **Break symmetry.** Left-align and let type sit with tension; avoid dead-centering everything.
- **One ownable motif** (the corner-cut), reused — not generic decoration.
- **One accent per slide.** More than one carnelian element and the restraint is gone.
- **Prefer real photography** over illustrated/graphic filler whenever you can get it.
- **Banned defaults:** gradient-mesh backgrounds, thin gold hairlines, glassmorphism, emoji in the headline, stocky thin-line icon sets used decoratively.

## 3. Format menu

Reuse these five layouts. Do not invent new ones; pour new content into existing forms.

| Format | Layout | Best for |
|--------|--------|----------|
| A | Editorial headline + angled product screenshot | Product, value prop |
| B | Stacked rows + thin-line icons + hairline dividers | Problem framing, education |
| C | Big-statement text slide | Hooks, positioning, quotes |
| D | Badge / CTA slide with carnelian button | Carousel closers, sign-up |
| E | Carousel: cover → 2–4 body slides → CTA | Education, storytelling |

## 4. Content pillars & target mix

Default toward the under-used pillars. Historically Scout content over-indexes on Product; the fresh ideas live in Proof, Education, and Community.

| Pillar | What it does | Target share |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Product | Show the thing working | ~25% |
| Proof | Real wins, stats, momentum | ~20% |
| Education | Standalone career value, useful even to a non-user | ~30% |
| Community | Cornell athletics culture, athlete & alum spotlights | ~15% |
| Build-in-public | The founder journey | ~10% |

Education is weighted highest because it earns follows and saves from people not yet ready to sign up. That is how the account grows past the people already searching for Scout.

For specific post ideas per pillar, read `references/angle-bank.md`.

## 5. Caption formula

Same skeleton every time, different substance:

1. **Hook** — first line, survives the "...more" cutoff (~80 chars). Curiosity, a sharp claim, or the start of a story. No "Hey guys."
2. **Body** — the payload: a 2–3 line micro-story or the concrete value. Short paragraphs. White space.
3. **One CTA** — rotate so it never feels naggy: Sign up / Tag a teammate / Save this / DM "Scout."
4. **Hashtags** — fixed core (`#Cornell #CornellAthletics #BigRed #StudentAthlete #CareerNetworking`) plus 2–3 rotating, woven in or at the end.

---

## 6. Batch workflow

Content is made in batches, not daily — batching is the biggest consistency lever.

1. Pick the slots (default cadence: Mon = Education/Proof, Wed = Product/Community, Fri = Build-in-public/Hook).
2. Pull one angle per slot from `references/angle-bank.md`. Never the same pillar twice in a row; never repeat an angle within 30 days.
3. Draft each caption with the formula in §5.
4. Run every caption through the voice rules in §1.
5. Specify the matching format (A–E) and the headline/slide art copy for each.
6. Output the batch as a clean list the user can hand to design and scheduling.

---

## 7. Output format

When asked for a single post, return:
- **Pillar / Format**
- **Art copy** (the headline and any slide lines that go on the image)
- **Caption** (hook, body, CTA as flowing text — no markdown emphasis)
- **Hashtags**

When asked for a batch, return N posts in that shape, spread across pillars with no back-to-back repeats, and note which format each uses.

Always end by stating which voice-rule risks you watched for, so the user can spot-check.
